### Abstract

This paper provides evidence that stock traders use round numbers as cognitive reference points for value. Analyzing over 100 million stock transactions, the authors find excess buying (selling) by liquidity demanders at price points just below (above) round numbers. The imbalance is monotonic in the “roundness” of the number — largest near integers, second-largest near half-dollars, and so on. Conditioning on price paths, stronger excess buying (selling) occurs when the ask falls (bid rises) to reach the integer rather than crossing it. Buy-sell imbalances also drive variation in 24-hour returns, with losses approaching $1 billion annually for liquidity demanders trading around round numbers.

### Key Ideas & Comments

- **Page 3, Paragraph 1**: Traders anchor on round numbers as psychological thresholds.
- **Page 3, Paragraph 2**: Buy-sell imbalances cluster around these thresholds, shaping short-term market behavior.
- **Page 5, Paragraph 2**: Integer effects are strongest, followed by half-dollars and smaller denominations.

This is a **classic introduction to the integer effect** in stock markets, with direct applications for developing trading signals and alphas across regions.

### Reflection

The study highlights how **behavioral biases** — in this case, fixation on round numbers — can systematically distort trading outcomes. It bridges psychology and market microstructure, showing that even tiny thresholds (like one penny below an integer) can aggregate into billion-dollar inefficiencies.
