---

## 2. What Structural Pattern Makes the Winners Good

The winning expression `rank(ts_mean(returns, 5))` succeeds because of three structural properties:

**A. Goldilocks Lookback (5 days)**
- Short enough to capture fresh momentum signals before they decay
- Long enough to filter out microstructure noise, overnight gaps, and single-day reversals
- The lessons file confirms: 3-day is "too noisy," 60-day is "sluggish" — 5 days sits in the sweet spot

**B. Rank Transformation**
- `rank()` converts raw return magnitudes into **cross-sectional percentiles**, making the signal:
  - Scale-invariant (doesn't depend on absolute volatility regimes)
  - Robust to outliers
  - Naturally bounded between 0 and 1, which pairs well with truncation at 0.05

**C. Subindustry Neutralization**
- The best settings use `neutralization: 'SUBINDUSTRY'` — the most granular option tested
- This strips out sector/subindustry beta, leaving only **idiosyncratic momentum**
- Aligns with WQ's documented practice of neutralizing at the most granular classification level available

**D. Moderate Decay (2)**
- `decay: 2` applies a light exponential smoothing — enough to reduce jitter without introducing dangerous lag
- `decay: 4` configurations show identical metrics here, suggesting the signal is relatively insensitive to decay in this range

---

## 3. Why the Losers Fail

**3-day lookback (all 8 configurations in your results):**
- **Noise dominance**: A 3-day mean of returns is hypersensitive to bid-ask bounce, overnight gaps, and intraday reversals
- **Low raw returns (0.058)**: Despite moderate Sharpe, the signal doesn't generate enough alpha to survive transaction costs
- **Self-correlation of 1.0**: This is a red flag — it suggests the signal is essentially static or has degenerate autocorrelation structure, meaning it's not actually producing meaningful time-varying positions
- All 8 parameter variations produce **identical metrics**, confirming the lookback is the binding constraint — no amount of parameter tuning can fix a fundamentally noisy input

**Longer lookbacks (40-60 days, from lessons):**
- **Sluggish response**: By the time the signal reacts, the momentum has already been arbitraged away
- **High self-correlation**: The signal barely changes day-to-day, leading to concentrated, stale positions
- **High turnover with low information ratio**: The 40-day lookback has 0.605 turnover — very high for a "slow" signal — suggesting it's churning without purpose

**10-day lookback:**
- **Ambiguous zone**: Not short enough to be fresh, not long enough to be stable
- High fitness (2.152) but lower Sharpe (0.938) suggests **possible overfitting** — the in-sample return/turnover ratio looks good but doesn't translate to robust risk-adjusted returns

1. Producing identical rankings every day (possible with very short lookbacks in certain market regimes)
2. Suffering from a data pipeline issue (e.g., stale returns data)
3. Being dominated by a small number of securities that always rank the same

**Key insight from lessons learned**: The 5-day window balances **signal freshness** (short lookback) against **noise suppression** (sufficient averaging). This aligns with the WQ alpha lifecycle principle of generating candidates that survive the quality gate.
