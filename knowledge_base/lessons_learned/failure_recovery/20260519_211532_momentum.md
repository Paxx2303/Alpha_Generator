We present explicit formulas – that are also computer code – for 
101 real-life quantitative trading alphas.  Their average holding

indirect empirical analysis.  We further find empirically that

## Applied Lessons
Looking at the failure, the core issue is **self-correlation of 1.00**, meaning the alpha is essentially identical to itself across time â€” likely because the two rank terms are highly redundant or one dominates completely.

**Diagnosis:**
- `rank(ts_mean(returns, 20) / ts_std_dev(returns, 60))` is a Sharpe-like momentum signal
- `rank(volume / ts_mean(volume, 20) - ts_mean(volume, 5) / ts_mean(volume, 60))` compares short-term to long-term volume
- The two components are likely collinear in certain regimes, pushing self-correlation to 1.00
- The deerflow pre-review FAIL suggests the expression structure itself is problematic (likely too similar to known alphas or has degenerate behavior)

**Remediation:**

1. **Break the collinearity** â€” use different lookback windows and orthogonal signals. Replace the return/volume overlap with a price-based mean-reversion component vs. momentum, which are naturally less correlated.

2. **Add zscore normalization** instead of pure rank â€” this reduces extreme co-movement in the tails that inflates self-correlation.

3. **Use ts_delta for momentum** rather than ratio of mean/std, which is smoother and less prone to regime-driven correlation spikes.

4. **Diversify volume signal** â€” use `ts_corr` between volume and returns as a confirmation signal rather than raw volume ratios, which adds an orthogonal dimension.

5. **Turnover control** â€” shorter lookbacks (5, 10, 20) mixed with longer ones (60) create natural rebalancing without excessive churn. The original 20/60 split was reasonable but the signal construction was redundant.

6. **Drawdown mitigation** â€” the zscore wrapper naturally clips extreme values, reducing tail risk. Adding a mean-reversion leg (negative ts_delta) provides a hedge during momentum crashes.
