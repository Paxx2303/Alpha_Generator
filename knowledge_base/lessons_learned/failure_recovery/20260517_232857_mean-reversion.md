Abstract 
We present explicit formulas – that are also computer code – for 
101 real-life quantitative trading alphas.  Their average holding 
period approximately ranges 0.6-6.4 days.  The average pair-wise 
correlation of these alphas is low, 15.9%.  The returns are strongly 
correlated with volatility, but have no significant dependence on 
turnover, directly confirming an earlier result based on a more 
indirect empirical analysis.  We further find empirically that 
turnover has poor explanatory power for alpha correlations.

The division by volatility without proper normalization or mean-reversion context likely amplifies noise and tail risk.

Based on the principles in *101 Formulaic Alphas*, effective signals often combine momentum with mean-reversion dynamics, use rank to ensure cross-sectional comparability, and incorporate volume or returns for robustness. To address the failures:

- **Sharpe & Fitness**: Replace raw delta/std_dev ratio with a z-scored version to stabilize variance.
- **Drawdown & Self-Correlation**: Introduce a short-term mean-reversion component via `ts_delta(returns, 1)` to reduce persistence and improve turnover.
- **Sub-universe Performance**: Use `ts_corr` with volume-weighted price to add orthogonal signal diversity.
- **Turnover Control**: Blend long-term momentum (`ts_delta(close, 60)`) with short-term reversal (`ts_delta(returns, 1)`) to balance holding period.

The improved expression uses `zscore` to normalize the core momentum signal, adds a volume-price correlation for orthogonal alpha, and includes a short-term return delta to manage drawdown and self-correlation.
